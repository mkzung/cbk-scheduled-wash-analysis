What the data says

  1. A scheduler, not demand. 75% of all trades and 60% of volume land on :05 and :35, about 22x a uniform clock: one trade every 90 seconds, alternating side, each within a ~127ms sub-second window. Two unrelated controls (GAIB, BOBA) stay flat near the 1.7% uniform line, so this is specific to CBK.
  2. Two-sided self-wash. Second :05 is 99% sells, second :35 is 99% buys; the 90-second cadence lands each side on its own second. The legs match: $129.7k bought vs $133.1k sold.
  3. Almost no net position, and it loses money. Over the month buying ($228.6k) and selling ($219.7k) are within about 4%. It pays the spread each round trip (buys ~0.4% above its sells), so it loses money rather than earning it like a real market maker: turnover without exposure.
  4. It hides its size, not its clock. The most common trade size is just 0.5% of trades (2,287 distinct sizes on the buy second), so it slips past clip and first-digit detection, but time-of-trade catches it.
  5. Read it as a flag, not a verdict: the time-of-trade and buy/sell signatures of a scheduled wash bot, not an attribution of intent.
